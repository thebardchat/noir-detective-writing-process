# Voice & Tone Guide

## The Detective's Voice

The book is written in noir third person. The detective narrates but is not above it — he's in it too. He is not a hero. He is not a philosopher. He is a man who notices things and files them somewhere important without knowing yet what they're for.

## Cadence Rules

### Short punches
One sentence. Sometimes one word. The punch lands and the silence after it is part of the sentence.

### Long runs
The sentence that keeps going because the thought keeps going because the observation is larger than a period can contain and the reader is carried by the momentum the way a passenger is carried by a car on a road that doesn't stop for punctuation.

### The alternation
Short. Then long. Then short again. The rhythm is jazz, not metronome. The reader feels the cadence before they understand the content. That's the trick.

## Humor

- Dry. Never announced.
- Placed where the reader doesn't expect it — between the heavy line and the heavier one.
- Self-aware without being self-conscious.
- The detective is funny because the world is absurd and he notices the absurdity without pointing at it.

## What the Voice Does NOT Do

- **Lecture.** The detective is not a professor. He doesn't explain the human condition. He drives through it.
- **Sermonize.** No moral of the story. No lesson. The reader finds the meaning. The detective just drives.
- **Judge.** The detective notices. There's a difference between noticing and judging that the detective is still working out, and that working-out is part of the voice.
- **Name people.** The detective is never named. The characters are described by what they do, not who they are. The garbage man. The old man. The small string one. Names are for people who need to be found. These people are already here.

## Callbacks and Echoes

The same image can appear in multiple scenes/tracks. When it does:
- Same image, different weight. The cupholder rattles in Scene 001 and it rattles in Track 001 but the rattle means something different because the detective is different.
- Never repeat a line verbatim unless the repetition IS the point.
- Echoes, not repetition. The reader should feel the callback before they identify it.

## The Raw Voice

Shane's speaking voice has its own cadence:
- Tangential by design — the tangent IS the scene sometimes
- Self-interrupting — new observations arrive mid-sentence
- Multilingual — fragments of other languages bleed through naturally
- Physical — the body is always present (driving, walking, eating, scanning)
- Emotionally honest without being emotionally aware — the feeling arrives before the name for the feeling does

The shaping preserves this. The noir prose is Shane's voice in a tuxedo — same person, different clothes.
