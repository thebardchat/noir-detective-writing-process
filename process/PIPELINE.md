# The Noir Pipeline

## How a Voice Dump Becomes a Track

### Stage 1: The Dump
Shane records while living. Driving home from work. Walking through a store. Sitting in a parking lot. The recording is raw dictation — no script, no outline, no plan. Just observation and thought moving at the speed of the road.

Raw dumps are messy by design. They contain:
- Observations about the world passing by (buildings, weather, people, signs)
- Internal monologue and philosophical tangents
- Interruptions (ordering food, scanning groceries, traffic)
- Multilingual fragments (the brain doesn't stay in one language)
- Emotional processing that arrives without warning
- References to things that won't make sense until three tracks later

The mess is the material. Don't clean it — mine it.

### Stage 2: The Read
Claude reads the raw dump and identifies:
- **The scene** — what is this about? Where are we? What's the image?
- **The feeling** — what arrived without warning? What's the thing underneath?
- **The voice** — which fragments are the real line? What did Shane say that he doesn't know he said yet?
- **The connections** — how does this link to existing scenes/tracks? What echoes?

### Stage 3: The Shaping
The raw material gets pressed into noir third person. Rules:
- **Stay tight to Shane's words.** The shaped version should be what he was thinking, in his cadence. Don't reimagine.
- **Find the punches and the runs.** Short lines land. Long lines carry.
- **Let the humor stay where it fell.** Don't move the jokes. They landed in the right place.
- **Connect the images.** Shane drops breadcrumbs across dumps. The shaping connects them.
- **Remove transcription noise, keep linguistic noise.** Fix garbled words. Keep the multilingual bleed-through — that's intentional.

### Stage 4: The Review
Shane reads the shaped version. Three outcomes:
1. **Yes** — the track is what he was thinking. Lock it.
2. **Redirect** — the track drifted from his head. Reshape.
3. **Riff** — the track sparked something new. More material incoming.

### Stage 5: The Lock
A locked track gets:
- Status updated to "Track" (from "Demo")
- Audio generated via ElevenLabs
- Placed in the album sequence

### Stage 6: The Compile
When enough tracks are locked, the album compiles. The order reveals itself. Side A and Side B take shape. The hidden track appears. The album tells you what it is.

## What Makes This Different

Most AI writing tools generate text from prompts. This pipeline generates text from **lived experience**. The AI never invents a character, a scene, or an emotion. It shapes what's already there — the observation that happened on a real road, the thought that arrived at a real traffic light, the feeling that landed in a real parking lot.

The human brings the life. The AI brings the craft. Neither is the author alone. Both are the author together.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Don't invent scenes.** If Shane didn't say it, it doesn't go in the track.
2. **Don't improve the voice.** Shane's cadence is the cadence. Don't make it more literary. Don't make it smoother. The roughness is the texture.
3. **Don't explain the metaphors.** If the image works, it works. Don't add a sentence telling the reader what it meant.
4. **Don't move the humor.** Shane's jokes land where they land. The placement is as important as the punchline.
5. **Don't force connections.** If two tracks connect, great. If they don't, don't stitch them together with glue the reader can see.
6. **Don't discard the foreign language.** When Hindi or Portuguese or Italian appears in a dump, it's the brain switching frequencies. That's meaningful. Interpret it, don't replace it.
